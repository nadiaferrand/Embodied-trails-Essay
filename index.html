Contribution to Knowledge

This paper rethinks infrastructure through the lens of feminist geography and embodied knowledge, centering the everyday practices of rural Kenyan women who navigate water access systems under conditions of scarcity and threat. Drawing on theorists such as Gillian Rose, Doreen Massey, Maurice Merleau-Ponty, William Bunge, Ursula K. Le Guin, Walter Benjamin, and others, the essay challenges technocratic understandings of infrastructure as neutral or abstract. Instead, it foregrounds the body as a site of spatial expertise, exploring how daily acts of walking, carrying, and negotiating water routes function as forms of resistance, authorship, and citizenship. By bridging critical theory with grounded, gendered practices of spatial navigation, this paper contributes to the field of city design by proposing an embodied and participatory framework for infrastructural planning—one that repositions care, improvisation, and lived experience as central to urban and rural design processes. Through a combination of philosophical reflection, theoretical comparison, and case study analysis, this work argues for a critical rewriting of space—one that moves from the map to the muscle, from policy to presence, and from data to lived experience.

Introduction

The body knows before the blueprint. It knows the routes that aren't on the map, the water points that don't show up in GIS systems, the wait, the weight, the wear. This essay begins from that embodied knowledge; often dismissed, often invisible, arguing not just for its inclusion in urban planning and geography, but for its centrality. Each morning in rural Kenya, women embark on familiar, physically demanding routes to fetch water. These acts are not merely tasks of survival; they are practices of spatial knowledge-making, shaped by care, caution, improvisation, and memory. Through each step, pause, and reroute, women read and rewrite infrastructure not as a static system but as a living terrain; contested, intimate, political. Drawing from feminist geography, urban theory, and infrastructural critique, this piece gathers a collective of thinkers whose ideas, like seeds, grow through the cracks of technocratic concrete: Massey (2005), Rose (1993), Le Guin (1986), Merleau-Ponty (1962), Fitzgerald, Warburg, Benjamin (1999). Each offers a different method, a different way of listening to the world. What emerges is not a smooth narrative, but a textured garden of theory and practice, resistance and care. At its heart, this essay asks: whose knowledge shapes our cities? And what forms of expertise have been erased in the name of efficiency, neutrality, or progress? By centering the embodied labor of women navigating broken or absent infrastructure, this work reframes planning not as a top-down science, but as a relational and lived practice. It is in footsteps, not forecasts, that space is most profoundly known.

Embodied Knowledge and the Feminist Rewriting of Space

Each morning, in rural Kenya, women rise before sunrise to begin the familiar yet strenuous journey to fetch water. These daily routes, often long, physically taxing, and shaped by negotiation with both terrain and social threat are not just acts of survival. They are practices of spatial knowledge-making. Through each step, pause, and reroute, women come to know infrastructure not as an abstract system, but as a living, contested, and embodied terrain. This is where feminist geography insists on a fundamental shift: from space as a technical object to space as lived experience. "The body," writes Gillian Rose, "is a crucial site of knowledge production" (Rose, 1993, p.142). For her, spatial understanding emerges through repeated engagement, touch, fatigue, rhythm, not detached analysis. The women navigating water routes in Kenya demonstrate this precisely with their bodies writing an alternative cartography with each journey. Their movements record infrastructural gaps, environmental changes, and informal solidarities—forms of expertise that remain illegible to top-down planning. Doreen Massey similarly rethinks space as "the product of interrelations," shaped through power and difference (Massey, 2005, p.10). Her prose flows like a river seeking its course—measured, systemic—while Rose's writing carries a sharper current of feminist refusal. Both theorists reveal what Merleau-Ponty articulates through his phenomenological lens: "The body is our general medium for having a world" (Merleau-Ponty, 1962, p.169). His philosophical abstraction, when woven with Rose's material feminism, provides the ontological soil in which embodied knowledge takes root. This embodied knowledge resonates with what bell hooks identifies as "the politics of location"—knowledge produced in the body, in place, through experience (hooks, 1990, p.145). While Massey theorizes from global scales and Rose anchors in poststructural critique, hooks brings us to the intimate ground of the everyday, her words alternating between academic precision and narrative fluidity. All three illuminate what Benjamin might call the "after-effects of the past"—how women's routes through Kenyan landscapes become living archives of both resistance and memory. Their footpaths are not simply practical responses to scarcity; they are, in Benjamin's sense, spatial texts where histories of drought, displacement, and resilience remain legible in every step. What unites these diverse thinkers—Rose's pointed feminist critique, Massey's relational geography, Merleau-Ponty's philosophy of perception, Benjamin's poetics of memory—is their shared commitment to unsettling technocratic knowledge. They challenge what counts as expertise through different registers: phenomenological, systemic, literary, historical. Meanwhile, rural Kenyan women do this work every day without footnotes, but with footsteps. Their bodies record what maps cannot: an archive of resilience, improvisation, and refusal written in muscle and bone. This is the grounded knowledge that feminist geography elevates, not as supplement, but as foundation—where theory meets earth, where abstraction touches skin.

Infrastructure Beyond Efficiency: Embodied Expertise and the Politics of Access

Urban planning has long been guided by a vocabulary of abstraction: efficiency, coverage, cost-effectiveness. In this technocratic framing, infrastructure becomes a system to be optimized; an engineering puzzle, a spreadsheet of inputs and outputs. But this language fails to account for how infrastructure is lived. It does not register the wait at the broken tap, the unsafe shortcut taken at dusk, or the bodily toll of daily water collection. For the women navigating these conditions in rural Kenya, infrastructure is not a passive backdrop—it is a terrain of struggle. Stephen Graham and Simon Marvin critique this imaginary with surgical precision. They argue that urban infrastructure has often been imagined as a "seamless, frictionless landscape of flow," masking the deeply uneven realities of access and use. Their writing, dense and diagnostic, dissects how "infrastructures are always imbricated with inequality", shaping cities not as cohesive wholes but as fragmented networks where "disconnection and exclusion are often engineered in." Ananya Roy sharpens this critique through a more political blade. Her concept of the "politics of access" reframes infrastructure from a technical system to a space of negotiation, where inclusion must be claimed rather than guaranteed. In rural Kenya, women's engagement with water systems enacts what Merleau-Ponty might recognize as "our general medium for having a world." The water point is not just a destination—it is a decision. When to go. Which route is safest? Whether there will be water. Whether it's worth the wait. Here, Rose's insistence that "the body is a crucial site of knowledge production" finds material expression in each woman's calculated risk, each detour, each shared warning about unsafe paths. These acts—quiet, repeated, embodied—form what Le Guin might celebrate as the "carrier bag" rather than the "hero's sword" of expertise: knowledge gathered, held, and shared rather than conquered or claimed. Benjamin's understanding of streets as places "where memory and history are manifest" speaks to how women's water routes become archives of both personal strategy and collective survival. It is through their eyes and their routes that a more just and responsive urbanism might emerge—one written not in master plans but in the intimate knowledge of terrain, time, and body that Massey calls "the product of interrelations" and what hooks names "the politics of location." Infrastructure, when viewed through this lens, becomes not merely a technical achievement but a lived negotiation—a daily poetry of adaptation written in footsteps rather than blueprints.

Embodied Routes as Spatial Authorship: Memory, Care, and Resistance

Daily acts of water collection in rural Kenya are not peripheral to infrastructure; they are its very condition of existence. As Awan, Schneider, and Till suggest, this is where spatial agency emerges: not in top-down intervention, but in the adaptive, improvisational work of everyday life. Women's routes, decisions, and routines reveal an infrastructural knowledge that remains unrecognized in dominant planning paradigms—a knowledge that bell hooks might locate in the politics of location and what Benjamin sees as the street becoming "the place where memory and history are manifest." These routes are not just practical, they are mnemonic infrastructures, embodying what Warburg's Mnemosyne Atlas suggests through its visual logic: space is not linear or empty, but layered, recursive, unfinished. These thinkers offer different registers: Le Guin's narrative care, Bunge's radical empiricism, Merleau-Ponty's phenomenology, Benjamin's poetics, Fitzgerald's participatory sociology—but they converge on the same question: who gets to define infrastructure? Sara Ahmed calls this affective economies: the idea that feelings and knowledge move unevenly through power. In the context of rural water access, the women who walk, wait, carry, and navigate are not simply recipients of infrastructure—they are its bearers. To return to their labor is to return to the politics of infrastructure. The path taken is a negotiation with terrain, safety, time, and community that constitutes what Lefebvre might call the right to the city. Jane Jacobs similarly argued cities work best when created by everybody. The most beautiful plans are not architectural; they are drawn daily, with the feet of those who inhabit them—writing what Massey calls "space as the product of interrelations."

Conclusion

This essay has argued that infrastructure is not merely built, it is inhabited, felt, and redefined daily through embodied practice. By centering the experiences of women who navigate rural Kenyan water routes, we uncover a form of infrastructural expertise that is not supplementary to planning, but foundational to it. Theoretical voices such as Rose, Massey, Merleau-Ponty, Fitzgerald, Bunge, and Le Guin help us articulate what these women already know: that space is produced not only through policy or design, but through movement, memory, and care. Technocratic design often erases these affective dimensions. In its place, this essay offers a feminist framework for spatial practice: one that values lived experience as data, routine as resistance, and adaptation as authorship. If the body knows before the blueprint, then planning must learn to listen differently. It must shift from consultation to co-creation, from efficiency to equity, from top-down maps to the muscle-memory of those who walk them. What emerges is not an idealized or smooth system, but a more honest and just one, rooted in the rhythms, refusals, and relational wisdom of those who move through space every day.
